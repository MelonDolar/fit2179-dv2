PART 1

A Warming Nation

Australia is one of the fastest-warming continents on Earth. Since 1910, the national average temperature has risen by more than 1.4°C — but the most alarming acceleration has happened in just the past decade.

The Bureau of Meteorology (BOM) records show that nearly every year since 2013 has ranked among the hottest in Australian history. Higher temperatures dry out vegetation, reduce soil moisture, and create the tinderbox conditions that fuel catastrophic fires.

2019 was the hottest year on record — a full 1.52°C above the long-term average. What followed was the worst bushfire season Australia had ever seen.

Source: Bureau of Meteorology, Annual Climate Summary, 2000–2023

PART 2

Where the Risk Is Greatest

Not all of Australia faces equal danger. A composite risk score — combining temperature increase, drought frequency, and burn history — reveals that the eastern seaboard and south-east are the most vulnerable. The size of each bubble represents the area burned in 2019–20.

▼ Scroll for all states

NSW — 8.4/10

Bore the greatest single-season burden in 2019–20: 5.49 million hectares burned and 2,448 homes destroyed. The Blue Mountains and South Coast were particularly devastated, with entire towns evacuated for weeks. Recovery costs exceeded A$2 billion.

ACT — 8.1/10

Despite its small size, the ACT's temperature has risen 1.5°C — the highest increase of any jurisdiction. In 2020, fires reached the outskirts of Canberra itself. The territory's dense bushland interface with suburban areas makes it uniquely vulnerable.

VIC — 7.8/10

1.5 million hectares burned in 2019–20. Victoria has seen its fire season extend earlier into spring each decade. The 2009 Black Saturday fires remain the deadliest in Australian history, killing 173 people and destroying over 2,000 homes.

QLD — 7.1/10

2.34 million hectares burned in 2019–20. Queensland's dry inland west is highly fire-prone year-round, but the 2019 season pushed fires into coastal regions unusually early — months before the traditional peak. Drought conditions across the state amplified the risk significantly.

SA — 6.9/10

Highest drought frequency of any state at 44% of years. Smaller fires but extremely dry fuel loads make ignitions spread rapidly. The Kangaroo Island fires of 2020 burned nearly half the island, destroying critical wildlife habitat and a thriving tourism industry.

WA — 6.5/10

3.2 million hectares burned, but vast remote areas mean lower impact on people and property. The south-west corner — including Perth's hinterland — faces growing risk as the region dries. Prescribed burn programs have helped reduce fuel loads in accessible areas.

NT — 5.2/10

4.8 million hectares burned annually in savanna fires, but most occur in remote areas with low population density. Indigenous ranger programs using traditional fire management — cool burns in the early dry season — have significantly reduced the area burned each year.

TAS — 5.8/10

Cooler and wetter than the mainland, but ancient pencil pine forests and alpine moorlands are irreplaceable once burned — these ecosystems took thousands of years to form and cannot regenerate within a human lifetime. Climate-driven drying is increasing their exposure.

Sources: BOM Climate Change in Australia; data.gov.au Bushfire Boundaries Dataset

PART 3

The Black Summer: 2019–20

No fire season in recorded history compares to the 2019–20 Black Summer. Fuelled by a record heatwave and the worst drought in decades, fires burned across every state simultaneously — an event with no modern precedent.

PART 4

A Season That Never Truly Ends

Traditionally, southern Australia's fire season ran from October to March. But rising temperatures and earlier spring warm-ups have eroded that boundary. Fire agencies now speak of a "year-round" fire risk in parts of Queensland and NSW.

The heatmap below shows the average Forest Fire Danger Index (FDI) for each month. December and January remain peak danger months, but the shoulder months of September–November and February–March are increasingly severe.

The FDI is a measure combining temperature, humidity, wind speed, and drought factor. An index above 50 is considered Severe. Above 75 is Extreme.

Source: Bureau of Meteorology, Forest Fire Danger Index climatology

PART 6

Drought Primes the Landscape

Rainfall deficit and burned area move in near lockstep. When rainfall falls well below average — as it did in 2018 and 2019 — vegetation dries out completely. Dry fuel loads then amplify any ignition into an unstoppable fire front.

The 2021 and 2022 La Niña years broke the drought cycle, and burned areas dropped dramatically. But 2023 saw rainfall deficit return, and with it, another surge in fire activity.

This connection is the key reason why climate scientists warn that an increasingly dry and variable Australian climate means more frequent catastrophic seasons — not just occasional ones.

PART 7

The Mounting Economic Toll

Bushfires impose staggering costs on the Australian economy. Property damage, loss of livestock and crops, and the enormous expense of emergency response add up year after year — with 2019 representing a shock of an entirely different order.

But even in "quiet" years, the cumulative cost is in the hundreds of millions. And as fire seasons intensify, insurance premiums, infrastructure hardening, and recovery programs will add pressure to federal and state budgets for decades.

Source: Deloitte Access Economics; AFAC National Situation Reports

PART 9

Every State Is Affected

No corner of Australia is exempt. The stacked area chart shows total incidents from 2015 to 2023 broken down by state. Queensland and Western Australia contribute the largest raw numbers due to their size, but per-capita impact in NSW and VIC is far higher.

The national total in 2019 was more than 35,000 incidents — nearly 50% above the average of surrounding years. The figure dropped in 2020–21 partly due to wetter conditions and partly because so much fuel had already been consumed.

PART 10

The Relationship Is Clear

When we plot temperature anomaly against total incidents, the correlation is unmistakable. Hotter years reliably produce more fires. The 2019 Black Summer is a dramatic outlier, but even within the "normal" range, higher anomalies consistently predict higher incident counts.

This relationship is what makes the future so concerning. Under current emissions trajectories, Australia is projected to experience temperatures 2–3°C above historical averages by mid-century. If that temperature-to-fire relationship holds, the Black Summer may become the new normal.

Sources: BOM Annual Climate Summary; data.gov.au Incident Records

What Can Be Done?

The data tells a clear story: Australia's fire crisis is not a random series of disasters — it is a predictable consequence of a warming climate. Understanding the scale and pattern of risk is the first step toward action.

🏠

Prepare Your Home

Clear gutters, seal gaps, install metal fly screens, and prepare a Bushfire Survival Plan with your household. Register with your local council's fire alert system.

📻

Stay Informed

Monitor fire danger ratings via the BOM website, your state's fire agency app (e.g., Fires Near Me NSW), and ABC Emergency radio during fire season.

🌱

Support Resilience

Back land management policies that incorporate First Nations burning practices, which have sustainably managed Australian landscapes for 60,000 years.

📢

Advocate for Change

Reducing fire risk in the long term requires reducing emissions. Contact your elected representatives and support climate and disaster risk policies.
